Why checking someone first actually matters

You're about to meet someone you only know from a chat. Maybe you're buying a phone from Facebook Marketplace, going on a first date, or agreeing to pay someone off in instalments.

Here's what we do, why it helps, and — just as importantly — what we can't promise.

The risk

You're trusting a stranger with very little to go on

A profile photo and a friendly chat don't tell you who someone really is. Most of the time, people are exactly who they say they are. But it only takes one bad experience — a no-show, a scam, or something worse — to turn a simple deal into a serious problem.

Checking someone's identity before you meet, pay, or sign anything takes less than a minute. It won't guarantee a good outcome, but it removes one of the biggest unknowns: are they even who they claim to be?

Why your rating matters

Rating someone isn't optional small talk — it protects the next person

When you rate someone after meeting them, you're not just leaving feedback. You're building a record that helps everyone who comes after you make a safer decision.

It can stop a repeat problem before it happens

If someone has behaved badly before, an honest rating or a flagged mismatch warns the next person — before they hand over money or open their door.

It rewards people who do right by others

A good rating gives credit where it's due. Reliable sellers, fair buyers, and trustworthy tradespeople build a Trust Score that opens doors — because their track record speaks for them.

It makes the whole community safer over time

Every rating adds to a shared pool of real experience. The more people rate honestly, the more reliable everyone's Trust Score becomes — for every transaction after yours.

So please — after you meet, trade, or hire someone, take 30 seconds to rate them. Whether the experience was great or went wrong, your honest rating helps build something bigger than your one interaction.

What we can — and can't — promise

Verification confirms documents. It doesn't confirm character.

We want to be completely upfront about what "verified" actually means here, so you know exactly how much weight to give it.

What "verified" really means

Our team checks that the ID document and the selfie belong to the same real person, and that the document looks genuine. That's it. We do not run background checks, criminal record checks, or confirm anything about someone's character, intentions, or honesty.

A verified badge means: this is a real person who exists and matches their documents. It does not mean they will behave well in your transaction.

  • Still meet in a safe, public place for the first time if you can
  • Still use secure payment methods, not upfront bank transfers to strangers
  • Still trust your own instincts — if something feels wrong, it's okay to walk away
  • Still tell someone where you're going if you're meeting in person

Think of verification as one good layer of protection — not the only one you need.

If things go wrong

The records we keep can support a real investigation

Every verification, every code that's checked, and every rating or mismatch report is logged with a timestamp. If something does go wrong — a deal turns into fraud, or someone isn't who they claimed to be — these records can help police or investigators establish who was involved and when. We can't prevent every bad outcome, but we make sure there's a trail if one happens.

Check before you meet. Rate after you do.

Both halves matter. Checking protects you today — rating protects everyone who comes after you.
